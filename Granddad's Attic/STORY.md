# Granddad's Attic - Story Source of Truth

## Premise

The player has inherited Granddad's attic. He always said he was "just an accountant" during the war, but the attic quietly says otherwise. Through five linked puzzles, the player discovers that Lt. Henry "Hank" Walsh served as a cryptanalyst at FRUMEL, the Allied codebreaking station in Melbourne.

The story should feel intimate and warm, not scary. The attic is a memory palace: dusty, sunlit, domestic, and full of small signs that a quiet man had a larger life than anyone knew.

## Core Emotional Turn

Granddad was not hiding treasure. He was hiding the truth because secrecy became a habit. The final reward is not a twist ending; it is the privilege of being trusted with what he never learned how to say out loud.

## Cast

- Henry "Hank" Walsh: Granddad. Former USNR lieutenant and FRUMEL cryptanalyst.
- Margaret Walsh: Granddad's wife. Her birthday, September 7, becomes the radio clue.
- The player: A grandchild or family member who cared enough to investigate.

## Story Beats

1. The player enters the attic and sees the footlocker, desk, coatrack, painting, and family photograph.
2. The wedding photo reveals the footlocker combination: 6.14.46.
3. The footlocker reveals a brass radio key and locked diary.
4. The diary's Caesar cipher lock opens with HONOR, decoded from KRQRU.
5. The diary reveals redacted date coordinates and Margaret's birthday.
6. The radio unlocks with the brass key and tunes to 97.0.
7. Granddad's recorded message points the player to the music box.
8. The music box melody spells BADGE.
9. The uniform jacket yields a badge with a Polybius square.
10. The diary date coordinates decode through the badge square to HONOR.
11. The painting slides aside to reveal the safe.
12. The safe opens with HONOR.
13. The player reads Granddad's final letter.

## Tone Rules

- Never make the attic horror-coded.
- Avoid melodrama in UI copy. Let objects carry the emotion.
- Granddad's voice should be plainspoken, wry, and restrained.
- The mystery is tender, not sinister.
- The final letter should feel like something written by a man who has avoided confession for sixty years.
